Grace Hopper

1906 - 1992

My Early Life & Achievements
I was an American computer scientist and US Navy rear admiral.
I was the first woman to earn a Ph.D. in mathematics from Yale University.
I created the first compiler named A-O for computer languages.
My compiler was the precursor for the COBOL language.
I became the first female recipient of the National Medal of Technology.
Due to my influence, programmers use “if/thens” instead of 1s and 0s today.
My Achievements
The naval ship USS Hopper was named in my honor.
I am called Grandmother of Cobol, Amazing Grace.
I inspired young people to learn to program.
I was honored with the Presidential Medal of Freedom.
At 79, I was the oldest serving officer in U.S Navy.
The Association for Computing Machinery offers a Grace Murray Hopper Award.
My Challenges
In my work on the compiler, I had to ingeniously design "forward jumps" in a program that had yet to be written.
After years of relentless effort, I spiraled into depression, alcoholism, and suicidal behavior.
I was rejected by the U.S. Navy for reasons that I couldn’t change.
I was deemed too short, too underweight, too old (37 years), and overqualified.
My Thoughts On Life
A ship in port is safe, but that's not what ships are built for.
It is often easier to ask for forgiveness than to ask for permission.
You don't manage people; you manage things. You lead people.
Humans are allergic to change. They love to say, "We've always done it this way." I try to fight that. That's why I have a clock on my wall that runs counter-clockwise.
To me programming is more than an important practical art. It is also a gigantic undertaking in the foundations of knowledge.
