Ruth Bader Ginsburg

1933 - 2020

My Early Life & Achievements
I was from Brooklyn, New York. I served as a Lawyer and Supreme Court Justice
My mother encouraged my learning by taking me to the library often.
I earned a bachelor’s degree from Cornell University with high honors and distinction in all subjects.
I was a member of the Harvard Law Review and the Columbia Review.
I was the first woman to receive tenure at Columbia University Law School.
I won the American Bar Association’s Thurgood Marshall Award for my contributions to gender equality and civil rights.
My Contributions
I was the second female justice appointed to the US Supreme Court
I was the first Jewish woman to serve in the US Supreme Court.
I believed that the law was gender-blind and all groups were entitled to equal rights.
I served as the director of the Women’s Rights Project of the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)
I argued six landmark cases on gender equality before the U.S. Supreme Court.
I was a leading advocate for the equal citizenship status of men and women.
My Challenges
My mother struggled with cancer and died the day before my high school graduation.
At Harvard, I balanced roles as a new mother, and a student in a hostile male-dominated environment.
Despite my stellar academic record, I encountered gender discrimination while seeking employment.
I contracted colon cancer despite which I did not miss a day of service on the bench.
Ten years later, I contracted pancreatic cancer, and was back in court within 12 days of my surgery.
My Thoughts On Life
My mother told me to be a lady. And for her, that meant be your own person, be independent.
Fight for the things that you care about, but do it in a way that will lead others to join you.
Even if you meet Prince Charming, be able to fend for yourself.
Your right to freedom of speech, your free exercise of religion stops at the other fellow’s nose.
Women belong in all places where decisions are being made.
