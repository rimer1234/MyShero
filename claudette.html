Claudette Colvin

1939 - Present

My Early Life & Achievements
I am an activist, a pioneer in the civil rights movement, and a nurse aide.
I was the first African American woman to refuse to move to the back of a bus, nine months before Rosa Parks did.
When I was 15, I refused to move to the back of the bus and give up my seat to a white person.
I was forcibly removed from the bus and arrested by two policemen and imprisoned.
My case was dropped by civil rights campaigners because: I was pregnant with a child out of wedlock, I did not have 'good hair', and was not fair skinned.
My Achievements
When I was young, we couldn't try on clothes in stores. You had to take a brown paper bag and draw a diagram of your foot and take it to the store.
I was charged with disturbing the peace, violating segregation laws, and battering and assaulting a police officer.
I am one of four women plaintiffs in Browder v. Gayle, the court case that successfully overturned bus segregation laws in Montgomery and Alabama.
March 2 is named Claudette Colvin Day in Montgomery.
My Challenges
I had difficulty finding and keeping work after my participation in the federal court case that overturned bus segregation.
I was branded a troublemaker by many in my community.
Furthermore, I withdrew from college and struggled in the local environment.
Though I was a predecessor to the Montgomery bus boycott movement, my story was rarely told.
NAACP believed that Rosa Parks would be a better figure since she was an adult, had a job, had a middle-class appearance and felt she had the maturity to handle being at the center of a controversy.
My Thoughts On Life
I knew then and I know now that, when it comes to justice, there is no easy way to get it.
You can't sugarcoat it. You have to take a stand and say "This is not right."
