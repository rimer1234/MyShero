Frida Kahlo

1907 - 1954

My Early Life & Achievements
I am a Mexican surrealist painter of the post-revolutionary Mexicayotl movement.
I am one of Mexico's most well-known and enigmatic cultural heroes.
I am renowned for painting about my experiences of chronic pain.
I was disabled by polio when I was a child.
I am also regarded as an icon for Chicanos, the feminism movement and the LGBTQ+ movement.
I am celebrated internationally for my uncompromising depiction of the female experience and form.
My Achievements
My home, La Casa Azul, is a popular museum with approximately 25,000 visitors monthly.
A park, Parque Frida Kahlo, featuring a bronze statue of myself, is dedicated to my name.
I am the first Hispanic woman to be honored with a U.S. postage stamp.
I was inducted into the Legacy Walk that celebrates LGBT history and people.
I was one of the inaugural honorees in the Rainbow Honor Walk, noting LGBTQ people who have made significant contributions in their fields.
My Challenges
I was disabled by polio when I was 6 years old.
In spite of a full recovery, the illness left my right leg thinner than my left. I wore long and beautiful dresses to conceal it.
I endured a bus accident that caused me lifelong pain and medical issues.
My marriage was plagued with infidelity and I suffered from anxiety, depression, alcoholism, and identity issues throughout my life.
All three of my pregnancies had to be terminated which caused me a lot of grief and heartache leading to depression.
